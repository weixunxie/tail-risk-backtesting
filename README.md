# Comparative Tail Risk Analysis of the S&P 500 and CSI 300

A quantitative risk analytics project comparing the downside tail-risk profiles of two major
equity indices using rolling-window VaR and CVaR estimation and Traffic Light backtesting.

---

## Project Overview

This project applies Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) methodologies
to daily price data for the S&P 500 (US equities) and CSI 300 (Chinese equities) over the
period January 2010 – December 2023. Three modelling approaches are evaluated at two
confidence levels, and a rolling-window backtesting framework is used to compare the
empirical performance of each model across both markets.

The **Python implementation** is the primary version of this project. R Markdown and MATLAB
implementations are preserved as supporting/legacy versions.

---

## Research Question

> Do standard VaR and CVaR models — calibrated on Historical, Normal, and EWMA-based
> volatility estimates — perform consistently across the S&P 500 and CSI 300 when subjected
> to rolling-window backtesting? How do their downside risk profiles differ?

---

## Motivation

VaR and CVaR are foundational tools in financial risk management, widely used by banks,
asset managers, and regulators. Comparing their behaviour across two major markets with
different structural characteristics (US vs. Chinese equities) provides insight into the
portability of standard risk models and highlights where distributional assumptions may
break down.

## Methodology

### Rolling Window

A 4-year estimation window (4 × 252 = 1,008 trading days) is used throughout.
For each observation in the test period (~2014–2023), VaR and CVaR are estimated
from the preceding 1,008 daily returns.

### Risk Models

| Model | VaR | CVaR |
|---|---|---|
| **Historical Simulation** | Empirical α-quantile of window | Mean of returns ≤ VaR |
| **Normal (Parametric)** | μ + Φ⁻¹(α) · σ from window | Closed-form normal tail expectation |
| **EWMA** (λ = 0.94) | Φ⁻¹(1−α) · σ_EWMA | Mean of all returns ≤ EWMA VaR |

Confidence levels: **95%** (α = 0.05) and **99%** (α = 0.01).

### Backtesting — Traffic Light System

Exception counts (days where realised return < estimated VaR or CVaR) are compared to the
expected count over the test period (~10 years × 242 trading days). Classification:

- **Green** — deviation ≥ −5% of expected (well-calibrated)
- **Yellow** — −15% ≤ deviation < −5% (moderately conservative)
- **Red** — deviation < −15% (over-conservative)

## Main Findings

The following findings are derived directly from the Python analysis (see notebook):

1. **VaR models are generally well-calibrated; CVaR models are systematically
   over-conservative.** Across both markets and both confidence levels, Historical,
   Normal, and EWMA VaR models achieve broadly acceptable Traffic Light ratings.
   CVaR estimates consistently produce fewer exceptions than expected.

2. **The S&P 500 and CSI 300 show different calibration profiles at 95%.** The
   Normal VaR model is well-calibrated for the S&P 500 (130 exceptions vs. 121 expected,
   Green) but over-conservative for the CSI 300 (94 exceptions, Red), suggesting the
   normality assumption fits the US market better at this confidence level.

3. **EWMA VaR produces the highest exception counts.** The EWMA model generates
   substantially more exceptions than expected (202 for S&P 500 at 95%), reflecting its
   tendency to widen estimates during volatile periods.

4. **At 99%, both indices perform comparably across VaR models.** Cross-market
   differences observed at 95% largely disappear at the deeper tail quantile, where all
   three VaR models receive Green ratings for both indices.

5. **Model choice matters more for the CSI 300 at 95%.** The CSI 300 shows wider
   variation in backtesting performance across models, underscoring the importance of
   model selection when applying risk frameworks to Chinese equity markets.

> **Note:** These findings depend on the specific Traffic Light convention implemented
> (deviation from expected exception count). Results are sensitive to the sample period,
> confidence level, and estimation window chosen.

## Limitations

- **Distributional assumptions** — The Normal model assumes i.i.d. Gaussian returns.
  Equity returns are typically leptokurtic with heavier tails, which may cause the Normal
  model to under-estimate tail losses at extreme confidence levels.
- **Sample-period sensitivity** — Results covering 2010–2023 include both a prolonged
  low-volatility regime and significant stress events (COVID-2020, interest-rate shock
  2022). Different sample periods would produce different backtesting outcomes.
- **Model risk** — No single VaR/CVaR model dominates across all regimes. Results should
  be interpreted alongside complementary risk measures (e.g., stress testing, scenario
  analysis).
- **Research scope** — This is an academic/educational project demonstrating quantitative
  risk methods. It is not a production risk system and does not constitute investment advice.
