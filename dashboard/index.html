Project Overview

Research Question

How do the S&P 500 and CSI 300 indices differ in their downside tail-risk profiles? Do standard VaR and CVaR models — calibrated on historical, parametric, and EWMA-based volatility estimates — perform consistently across both markets when subjected to rolling-window backtesting?

Methodology

Risk Estimation Framework

For each observation in the test period, VaR and CVaR are estimated using a trailing 4-year window (1,008 trading days). Three modelling approaches are applied at two confidence levels (95% and 99%).

Historical Simulation

Uses the empirical quantile of the rolling window to estimate VaR. CVaR is the mean of returns that fall below the VaR threshold. No distributional assumption.

Normal (Parametric)

Assumes returns within the window are normally distributed. VaR is derived from the inverse normal CDF; CVaR uses the closed-form normal tail expectation formula.

EWMA (λ = 0.94)

Exponentially weighted moving average variance, following RiskMetrics convention. EWMA adapts quickly to volatility clustering by down-weighting older observations.

Traffic Light Backtesting

Exception counts are compared to expected counts over the test period (~10 years). Ratings: Green (well-calibrated), Yellow (moderate deviation), Red (>15% below expected).

Results

Key Findings

  1. 1
    VaR models are generally well-calibrated; CVaR is systematically over-conservative.
    Across both markets and both confidence levels, Historical, Normal, and EWMA VaR models achieve Green or near-Green Traffic Light ratings. CVaR estimates consistently produce fewer exceptions than expected, suggesting the models over-estimate the magnitude of tail losses beyond the VaR threshold.
  2. 2
    The S&P 500 and CSI 300 show different calibration profiles at 95%.
    At the 95% confidence level, the Normal VaR model is well-calibrated for the S&P 500 (Green: 130 exceptions vs. 121 expected) but over-conservative for the CSI 300 (Red: 94 exceptions). This divergence may reflect the CSI 300's heavier tail structure, which the Normal model does not capture adequately.
  3. 3
    EWMA VaR produces the highest exception counts.
    The EWMA model generates substantially more exceptions than expected at both confidence levels (202 for S&P 500 at 95%, 88 at 99%), consistently receiving Green status. This reflects EWMA's tendency to widen estimates during volatile periods, producing a less conservative daily bound that is breached more frequently.
  4. 4
    At 99%, both indices perform comparably across VaR models.
    The cross-market differences observed at 95% largely disappear at 99%, where all three VaR models receive Green ratings for both indices. This suggests that at deeper tail quantiles, the distributional differences between the two markets become less operationally significant for this backtesting framework.
  5. 5
    Model choice matters more for the CSI 300 at 95%.
    The CSI 300 shows more variation in backtesting performance across models at 95% (ranging from Green to Red), while the S&P 500 is more consistent. This underscores the importance of model selection when applying Western-calibrated risk frameworks to Chinese equity markets.

Caveats

Limitations

Distributional Assumptions The Normal model assumes i.i.d. Gaussian returns. Equity returns are typically leptokurtic with heavier tails, which can cause underestimation of extreme losses.
Sample-Period Sensitivity Results covering 2010–2023 include both a prolonged low-volatility regime and significant stress events. Different sample periods would yield different backtesting outcomes.
Model Risk No single VaR/CVaR model dominates across all regimes or confidence levels. Backtesting performance should be read alongside complementary risk measures.
Research Scope This is an academic/educational project demonstrating quantitative risk methods. Results do not constitute investment advice or a production-grade risk system.
